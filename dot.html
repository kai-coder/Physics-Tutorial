Dot Product

The dot product is used to see how much of a vector is in the direction of another vector (known as the scalar product) and its just one simple equation:

a.x * b.x + a.y * b.y = |a| * |b| * cos(θ)

I know that looks quite scary, so let's break it down. The symbol θ is just the angle between the two vectors. Also, |a| just means the length of vector a (if you thought it was absolute value you're not alone).

So how does this equation tell us the information we need?

Before we get into that, let's substitute a for the difference between the velocities (shown in dark purple) and b as the direction of the collision normal (the short yellow vector). Because vector b is normalized, meaning it has the length of 1, we can just remove |b| from the right side of the equation because it has absolutely no effect on the product. So now the equation looks more like this: 

a.x * b.x + a.y * b.y = |a| * cos(θ)

If you look at the demonstration, you can see that if we drew a line between the dot product and the velocity difference, it forms a right triangle. You will also notice that the velocity difference is the hypotenuse of that right triangle.

For those who don't know cos(θ) in a right triangle equals the length of the side (that is not the hypotenuse) next to the angle θ, known as adjacent, divided by the length of the hypotenuse. Go to cosine rule for further explanation.

Now by looking at the demonstration, you can also see that the scalar product is the adjacent side of the angle θ. You can now probably see where this is going. Since the equation of cos(θ) is adjacent / hypotenuse or in our case scalar / |a|, if we multiply |a| by cos(θ), we will get the scalar product.

This scalar product is useful because when scaling (thats why scalar sounds like scale) the normal by the scalar, it results in a vector that is in the direction that we need to change the velocity in.
